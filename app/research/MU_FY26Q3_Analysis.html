Q1 — SCA floor price coverage vs. 40% revenue target
"Sanjay, I think we are all trying to figure out how much is locked in in kind of a floor price scenario over the next five years. There were two things you said. You said that 14 of the 16 SCAs have $100 billion in cumulative revenue. That sort of says like $20 billion a year at a floor price, which is way below the run rate that you just guided. That says that not that much would be covered at a floor price. You also said that 40% of revenue will be moving inside of these SCAs. Can you maybe double-click on all that and sort of help us in a floor price scenario? Can you help us think about how much of revenue per year would be guaranteed?"
Sanjay Mehrotra — CEO
"As we indicated that under these SCAs that have been completed so far, at the floor price, the revenue is projected to be $100 billion. Again, as Mark noted in his remarks, we expect revenue to be much higher than that. Note that at the floor price, our profitability levels at the gross margins are higher than peak margins at any time in the past. Overall, about 20% of DRAM and about 30% of our NAND volume is covered in these SCAs so far. That comes close to about 25% of our revenue that you can project over the term of these agreements. Again, RPO at the floor price is to be reported as an accounting measure, but we fully expect that the revenue will be much higher than that."
⚑ Logic Gap
The analyst correctly identified that $100B ÷ 5yr = $20B/yr — far below the $165B annualized run rate. Management deflected by emphasizing floor ≠ expected revenue and reframing around gross margin superiority, without addressing the small scale of guaranteed coverage relative to current run rate.
Q2 — How do SCAs layer into the quarterly model?
"With respect to just how these layer in, Mark, how much of the August quarter revenue, for example, will be flowing under an SCA? I'm just trying to figure out how to layer that into the model. When you get to a full run rate — by next fiscal Q4, will you be at sort of a full run rate of what's being covered under these SCAs?"
Mark Murphy — CFO
"Yeah, Tim. You'll see a disclosure in the Q, which will disclose the next 12 months revenue associated with each set of agreements that have an RPO. For example, for those that closed within Q3, you'll see an RPO of over $5 billion, and you'll see a next 12 months associated with that of about $1.8 billion. That is because those are some of the smaller agreements — automotive agreements. In the fourth quarter, you will see an RPO reported on 14 of the 16 agreements. That is going to be about $100 billion. There will be an associated next 12 months that will be disclosed in the Form 10-K. This RPO number is a minimally contractually enforceable amount for the intersection of volume and price — a minimum number. We were clear that it doesn't reflect what we think will happen. Also, each quarter this RPO number will change based on contracts added, additional volume commitments with a determined price, and shipments as performance obligations are met. Even at the floor price, eventually we anticipate about 40% of our revenue being under RPO-related commitments — and even at the floor price, we expect margins to be significantly above prior peak margins."
⚑ Logic Gap
Mark withheld the "next 12 months" figure for the $100B RPO, deferring to the 10-K. If the automotive conversion ratio applies (~36%: $1.8B/$5B), the implied run-rate for $100B would be ~$36B/yr — but management would not endorse that extrapolation, providing the breadcrumbs while refusing to bake the bread.

Q3 — Role of the $22B cash deposit
"I also wanted to ask about the LTAs. Can you talk about the role of the cash deposit? Should we think of that as being sort of an escrow collateral account, where if people cancel, you would have access to cash? If it's not revenue, what is the point of the deposit, and what is the relationship of those deposits with the RPO, if there is one?"
Mark Murphy — CFO
"Yeah. Joe, on the deposits. We have $22 billion of deposits and financial commitments associated with the agreements signed to date. $18 billion, approximately, is cash deposits. We received about $400+ million in the third quarter. We will receive about another $10 billion in the fourth quarter. These will be seen in financing cash flows — they will not affect free cash flow. They are held by us during the performance commitments of the agreements, and as those agreements are satisfied, those deposits will be returned over time, heavily weighted to the back half of the agreements. The difference between the $22 billion and the $18 billion — roughly $4 billion — is letters of credit."
Q4 — If deposits are returned, what is their binding force?
"Okay. What is the role? What happens to that cash? It seems like they're putting a deposit, and then they get the deposit back. What is the reason for them to commit that cash? Is that something where there's a take or pay that cash is related to? It's not a prepayment. Can you help us understand that?"
Mark Murphy — CFO
"Yeah. Thanks, Joe. It's not a prepayment. It's a separate commitment by the customers and a reflection of the fact that we have a binding agreement, and these are take or pay agreements. We hold the cash, and it's a reflection of our shared commitment to perform under these agreements. This is good for Micron — we get visibility on our demand, committed volume that we can be confident about, enabling large capital investments and a closer technology relationship. It's good for the customers because they have supply assurance and access to leading technology. In our view, it's a win-win. We are very happy with the nature of the agreements and the indication of a transformed business model at Micron."
⚑ Critical Logic Gap
The entire exchange never answered: (1) what happens to the deposit if a customer defaults on take-or-pay; (2) whether the letters of credit represent default penalties or performance bonds; (3) the legal relationship between deposits and RPO obligations. Mark described deposits as a "shared commitment" — moral language, not legal enforcement language. If deposits must be returned regardless of default, the actual binding force of take-or-pay remains unverified.

Q5 — Deposit fungibility and capital return planning
"To just follow up on Joe's question, Mark, when you think about these cash deposits, do you view that as fungible cash usable for CapEx? And when you contemplate capital returns, particularly after the December 9th CHIPS Act end date, will you include that cash in your gross cash thoughts? Is that something — given that you will have to return it eventually — that would cause you to think you'll need to hold more gross cash all else equal?"
Mark Murphy — CFO
"CJ, it's unrestricted. [On capital return:] Not in the near term. We're going to have what we view as adequate liquidity to support the operation of the business. That would include, over time, returning the deposits as customers and Micron perform on the contracts. Then we would hold liquidity to satisfy what investments we believe are important — large projects underway to provide supply and R&D programs. Again, I'll emphasize that customers will get this deposit back in the latter half of the agreement."
⚑ Observation
The $18B in cash deposits is effectively an interest-free, no-restriction loan from customers. Micron can deploy it for CapEx immediately. The repayment liability is back-loaded to 2028–2030. This is an uncommonly favorable financing structure for a capital-intensive company — and materially lowers Micron's effective cost of capital for this expansion cycle.
Q6 — HBM market share and margin trajectory
"Maybe as a follow-up on HBM revenues, could you share how you're thinking about both your market share and perhaps total revenues into calendar 2026? Is there an expectation into calendar 2027 that you can bridge margins there closer to what you're getting on DDR5? Or is that a place that will be permanently below that DDR5 level?"
Sanjay Mehrotra — CEO
"With respect to HBM, very pleased with our HBM4 product — shipments already over $1 billion. HBM market share, we strategically are choosing it to be close to our DRAM share. This is important because of the trade ratio of HBM. It consumes a significant amount of wafers and puts pressure on non-HBM supply in the industry. Targeting our HBM share close to our DRAM share (~24%) strategically enables us to supply our diversified end markets — data center, consumer, automotive, industrial. Regarding pricing for next year, we are not commenting on pricing. Certainly, HBM is a product where Micron has a strong leadership position. It is a higher price product compared to non-HBM on a per-bit basis, and it does provide strong ROI."
⚑ Logic Gap
HBM gross margin relative to DDR5 was never disclosed. If HBM margin is lower due to TSV packaging costs, the strategic decision to cap HBM share at ~24% may also serve to protect blended gross margins — a possibility management did not address. The "strong ROI" framing is deliberate vagueness.

Q8 — Long-term margin normalization to mid-70s%?
"Mark, on gross margins — 86%, does it hang out here for a while? As your long-term investors build their models for 2027, 2028, should they be assuming a normalized gross margin range somewhere in the mid-70s, right? The range between where you are today versus the prior peak in the low 60s?"
Mark Murphy — CFO
"Yeah. Vivek, we're not providing guidance beyond the fourth quarter. We are at margin levels that, as we've talked about before, incremental price yields less in gross margin expansion. Having said that, we do see the market remaining tight beyond 2027. Memory is very much appreciated for the strategic asset that it is. Our continued deployment of bits to data center and higher performance applications is going to be helpful as price growth moderates. Also, we will get additional volume starting materially mid-year 2027, that will grow into 2028. We will have some startup costs there, but we will get absorption as those ramps occur. Over time, we'll get that operating leverage. We feel great about the trajectory of the business and Micron's technology position."
⚑ Logic Gap: 2027H2 Dual Pressure Window
Mark disclosed — under the cover of "not providing guidance" — that both startup costs (ID.1 Idaho + Hsinchu Taiwan, mid-2027) AND FY27 OpEx increase of ~$1B (weighted to 2H) converge on 2027H2. Even if supply/demand stays tight, 2027H2 may be a cyclical trough quarter for gross margins. This information was extracted by the question rather than volunteered. Models should treat 2027H2 conservatively.

Q9 — Implied SCA pricing range: floor ~$10–12/GB, ceiling ~$25/GB
"Congrats on the great results. On the floor pricing for the LTAs — your prior peak gross margin was somewhere in the low 60s, ~62%. If I try to plug in what a 64 GB server DRAM is, I can get like a $700 price for it compared to $1,500 today, which puts you at ~$10–$12 a gigabyte as the floor and a mid-$20 a gigabyte for the current price. Is that the range we should think about for these LTAs — low teens to mid-$20 a gigabyte?"
Sanjay Mehrotra — CEO
"Krish, we are not going to get into specific pricing discussions, but I just want to note again that the gross margins at the floor will be well beyond the peak that we experienced in the past cycle. Well beyond those. We are not going to get into the specifics related to the pricing. Bottom line is, these SCAs really help provide visibility, strength, and durability of demand for us, and they absolutely fundamentally accelerate our financial performance and the business transformation here."
⚑ Analyst Implied Pricing Logic
Krish's calculation is the most rigorous public estimate of SCA pricing: if prior-peak GM ≈ 62% and floor GM is "well beyond" that (~68–75%), reverse-engineering 64GB server DRAM pricing gives a floor of ~$10–12/GB and ceiling of ~$25/GB (current CQ2 price). Management declined to confirm but also did not refute. Refusing to confirm specific numbers while acknowledging the logic is itself a form of information management.
Q10 — Quantifying supply-demand imbalance in 2027
"Just a quick follow-up. You mentioned DRAM bits should grow low to mid-20s%, NAND probably 20% this year — clearly we are undersupplied on both. Is there a way to quantify what happens in 2027? Is the undersupply going to be double what it is this year in 2027? Or how to think about the supply-demand imbalance in 2027?"
Sanjay Mehrotra — CEO
"We see 2027 overall tight. We have said we see tightness continuing beyond 2027. Working hard to bring up supply. It takes a long time to bring up the additional capacity needed — additional wafer capacity — and technology transitions with less bit gain per node, as well as the HBM trade ratio, put tremendous pressure on overall supply growth. Supply, even in 2028 when it begins to improve gradually, will face a demand trajectory that remains very strong because these AI trends are very long-term. AI is still in very early innings. The whole token economics needs more memory. AI system performance is really very much limited by memory capacity and bandwidth. The demand for memory grows as compute demand grows and our customers continue to make investments like they have never made before to build this infrastructure. The demand trajectory is extremely strong. Memory is at the center of it and is a strategic asset. We are working hard to bring up supply. We see tightness persisting beyond 2027."
⚑ Observation
Sanjay explicitly declined to quantify the 2027 imbalance even directionally. This refusal — while Micron is also the largest capacity expander at $27B annual CapEx — is strategically consistent: disclosing that undersupply deepens would validate ever-higher spot prices, which management may prefer to let the market discover independently. The "supply persists tight beyond 2027" framing is the softest possible confirmation without a quantified claim.
SCA Contract Architecture

Contract Scale

  • 共 16 個協議（已完成）
  • 目標：完成後 50%+ 營收
  • 現況：~25% 營收（16個SCA）
  • 含固定/上限定價：~40% 營收
  • 大型客戶 ×4，中型 ×3，汽車 ×9
  • DRAM 含 HBM 供應（視客戶需求納入）
  • 次世代產品（LP6 / DDR6 / 新HBM）溢價另行協商

Contract Terms

  • 資料中心/消費：5年（2026–2030）
  • 汽車：3年
  • 14/16 個協議有 floor price
  • 部分固定價格或無價格區間

Financial Commitments

  • 客戶押金+承諾：$220億
  • 現金押金：~$180億（無限制）
  • 信用狀：~$40億
  • Q3 已收：$4億+，Q4 預收 ~$100億

RPO (Minimum Purchase Obligations)

  • Q3 末（汽車）：>$50億
  • Q3 關聯12個月：~$18億
  • 14個大SCA總RPO：~$1,000億
  • ASC 606 會計準則揭露
  • 為最低值，非預期值

Take-or-Pay Enforceability — Core Questions

合約名義上是「強制購買，不買也要付費」，但法說會中完全未揭露：

① 若客戶不提貨，Micron 的救濟手段為何？（訴訟？押金沒收？）

管理層用「道德承諾」的語言描述押金，而非法律強制性語言，是本次法說會最需要追問的未解問題。

Long-Term Demand Scenarios: Auto × Humanoid Robots × Edge AI

L2+ 自動駕駛：記憶體含量為一般車輛 5倍以上。L2+ 滲透率 2026年翻倍突破 20%，預計 2030 年超過 40%；1-gamma LPDDR5 已達汽車產品就緒，DDR5 樣品已交 robotaxi 客戶。

人形機器人：搭載記憶體為 L2+ 車輛 10 倍。Sanjay 預言「本十年末開始的多十年記憶體需求週期」，視之為繼 AI 資料中心之後最大的長期結構性需求驅動力。

Edge AI / Agentic AI：OpenClaw、NemoClaw 等 edge AI 平台推動 PC 與手機高端化，per-device 記憶體含量持續提升，即使全球出貨量下降，產業營收仍可成長——這正是 MCBU 毛利率能達到 87% 的結構性支撐。

Risk Matrix: Disclosed vs. Undisclosed
High
Take-or-Pay Enforcement Opacity

High
Ceiling Price Fixed at Historic High
If market prices keep rising, the ~25% of revenue under SCA cannot benefit. Micron traded away future upside for certainty — a tradeoff management never directly acknowledged.
High
Self-Fulfilling Supply Scarcity Narrative
Micron is the largest beneficiary and also the largest capacity expander ($27B annual CapEx). The "supply doesn't improve until 2028" thesis carries strong management incentive bias — independent verification needed.
Med
HBM Share Strategy Opportunity Cost
Voluntarily capping HBM share may forfeit the highest-growth segment in AI accelerators. If competitors (SK Hynix) expand HBM share, Micron's long-term position in the AI supply chain erodes.
Med
Customer Deposit Liquidity Illusion
The $18B cash deposit is "unrestricted" but remains a liability (must be returned). Heavy return obligations in the back half of agreements, coinciding with CapEx ramp, could create liquidity pressure.
Med
Geopolitical Risk Excluded from Guidance
Guidance explicitly excludes any trade or geopolitical impact. China is a major memory market — any escalation in export controls (e.g. HBM) would directly hit undisclosed portions of revenue.
Med
FY2027H2 Dual Pressure: Startup Costs + OpEx Spike
Management guided: ① ID.1 (Idaho) + Hsinchu fab ramp mid-2027 with significant startup costs; ② FY27 OpEx up ~$1B weighted to 2H. Both converge in 2027H2. Even if supply/demand stays tight, 2027H2 gross margin may be a cyclical trough. A market misread as trend reversal rather than one-time pressure is a tail risk worth monitoring.
Low
CHIPS Act Capital Return Restriction (until Dec 9, 2026)
Micron 的大規模股票回購計畫受 CHIPS Act 限制，需等到 2026 年 12 月 9 日（簽署 CHIPS 定義協議兩周年）後才能擴大。在此之前，超額現金累積速度雖快（FQ3 淨現金 $244億），但回流股東的規模受限。2026/12/9 後管理層明確目標長期回流 100% 超額現金，屆時可能是催化劑。
